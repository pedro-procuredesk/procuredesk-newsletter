Your Q1 Books Aren't Clean Yet — Here's What's Still Hiding in Your PO Accruals

Goods arrived in March. The invoice hit in April. Your Q1 P&L already posted the wrong number — and the clock is ticking before it becomes a Q2 problem too.

⚠️ Time-sensitive  ·  Fix this before Q2 close begins

Q1 is done, but for most mid-market finance teams, the books aren't actually clean yet. The quiet failure: goods received in March with invoices that won't arrive until April or later. By the time they post, they land in the wrong period. Q1 understates expenses. Q2 opens with a correction that disrupts your variance analysis before it even starts — and if you're running 50 to 100 POs a month, there are likely more of these mismatches than you think.

"The problem isn't that invoices are late. It's that your accrual process has no way to see them coming — so you're always cleaning up after the fact."

The fix isn't complicated, but it requires connecting the right data before Q2 close begins. When the purchase order, the goods receipt, and the vendor invoice live in one platform, your received-not-invoiced list is accurate and actionable — not assembled from warehouse emails and spreadsheet guesses. Four things to get right right now:

1
Unified PO & Receipt Tracking
Link every PO to its goods receipt in one system — no spreadsheet reconciliation
  
2
Automated 3-Way Matching
Match PO, receipt, and invoice automatically — flag discrepancies before they post
3
Real-Time RNI Report
Get the received-not-invoiced list on Day 1 of close — built from confirmed data, not estimates
  
4
Clean Reversal Integrity
Accruals reverse correctly in Q2 — no negative expense balances, no audit surprises

● Operator's Checklist

7 Process Fixes to Make Before Q2 Close Catches You Again

Q1 exposed the gaps. Q2 doesn't have to repeat them. These are the structural fixes that stop the same problems from coming back next quarter — not another band-aid.

01Set up auto-reversing accrual entries in your ERP. If you posted manual Q1 accruals, make sure the reversals are scheduled for April 1. A missed reversal means Q2 double-counts the expense when the real invoice posts.
02Require goods receipts to be logged in the system — not emailed. An email from the warehouse confirming delivery is not a receipt record. If your process still relies on those, you'll be back in the same position at Q2 close.
03Create a standing received-not-invoiced (RNI) report. This should be a live report you can pull at any time — not something your team assembles from scratch at quarter end. If it takes more than 5 minutes to generate, it's not a report yet.
04Block invoice processing without a PO number. Every invoice that bypassed the PO process in Q1 is a manual reconciliation problem. Set the rule now: no PO, no payment. Make the exception require an approval, not a workaround.
05Set a hard close date for PO receipts — not just invoices. Most teams have an invoice cutoff. Fewer have a receipt cutoff. Without one, your accrual register is always incomplete on Day 1 of close.
06Cancel or close any POs that are 60+ days old with no receipt. These inflate your committed spend and muddy every report that touches open obligations. Stale POs are clutter — clean them up now while Q2 is early.
07Write down your accrual methodology — this week, while it's fresh. Whatever your team did to close Q1, document it in plain language. The next close runs faster when your process isn't reconstructed from memory under deadline pressure.

● From the CEO's Desk
S

Sachin Sharma

Founder & CEO, ProcureDesk

Scaling companies usually land in one of two broken states. The first is what I call the CF-No state — ten approvals for a single purchase, total control theater, and zero actual compliance because people find workarounds just to get their jobs done. The second is the opposite extreme: everyone buys what they need, trust is assumed, and finance has no idea where the money is going. Both feel like a choice. Neither is a process. What's actually worked across hundreds of growing companies I've worked with is building toward the guardrail state. This is where 80% of spend follows the rules automatically, your team moves fast, and finance isn't babysitting every transaction. You stop building manual gates that halt work and start building systemic guardrails that guide it. Speed and control aren't a tradeoff — they're the same thing when the system is right.
