● Operator's Checklist

7 Things to Fix Before You Add Procurement Software

Software doesn't fix a broken process — it accelerates it. Run through this list before you evaluate, buy, or implement anything new.

01Document your current approval workflow. If you can't draw it on a whiteboard in 5 minutes, your team can't follow it — and the software won't fix that ambiguity for you.
02Clean your vendor master list. Duplicate vendors, outdated contacts, and unapproved suppliers are data problems — and they migrate directly into any new system you install.
03Define your spend categories. If every department uses different naming conventions for the same type of purchase, your reporting will be noise. Standardize before you go live.
04Confirm your ERP integration requirements early. Which data needs to flow where, in what format, and at what frequency. Finding this out after contract signature costs weeks.
05Identify your internal champion. Procurement software without an internal owner stalls in week three. Name one person, give them the mandate, and make sure leadership is visible behind it.
06Set your baseline metrics now. PO cycle time, invoice processing cost, approval turnaround. If you don't have the before number, you can't prove the after — and you can't justify renewal.
07Align finance and operations on what success looks like. Finance wants visibility and control. Operations wants speed and less paperwork. Both are valid — and a good implementation delivers both. Get agreement before kickoff, not after.

● From the CEO's Desk
S

Sachin Sharma

Founder & CEO, ProcureDesk

A preferred vendor list is only as good as the last time someone actually reviewed it. Most finance teams build one, feel good about it, and move on — but vendor relationships get comfortable. Three years later nobody's asked the uncomfortable question: is this vendor still earning their spot? Either the vendor grew and their service quality dropped while the price stayed flat, or the market moved and better options appeared but nobody went looking. A preferred vendor list is a starting point, not a destination. The teams that actually protect their margins treat it like a quarterly decision, not a one-time setup. If no specific person owns that review with a specific cadence, the list might be costing you more than it's saving.

Corporate Cards vs. Purchase Orders: Why Mid-Market Finance Teams Need Both

Finance teams often treat this as an either-or decision. It isn't — and getting it wrong leaves a significant slice of spend either uncontrolled or untracked.

DimensionCorporate CardsPurchase Orders
Best forLow-value, high-frequency spend (T&E, SaaS, office supplies)Planned vendor spend with invoices, contracts, or delivery schedules
Spend visibilityPost-spend — you see it after it's chargedPre-spend — committed amount is visible before the invoice arrives
Approval controlCard limits and spend policies enforce guardrailsApproval workflows enforce guardrails before any money moves
Month-end closeRequires manual coding and receipt reconciliation3-way match automates most of the reconciliation work
Coverage gapMisses vendor invoices, project spend, and anything PO-drivenMisses card spend, subscriptions, and employee-initiated purchases

Cards control what happens after the swipe. POs control what happens before a vendor is contacted. Neither covers the full picture alone — and the gap between them is where untracked spend accumulates.
