📋 2026 Guide for Controllers

Three-Way Invoice Matching for Manufacturing — Without an ERP

Most matching guides assume you run SAP. This one doesn't. A 5-step implementation for Controllers on QuickBooks, Sage Intacct, or Business Central.

📋 Built for mid-market finance teams

A 160-person manufacturer got handed a $280,000 ERP quote. The problem it was supposed to fix? Three-way invoice matching. She didn't buy it — and she was right not to. Three-way matching isn't an ERP feature. It's a process: the PO, the goods receipt, and the vendor invoice all have to agree before a payment goes out. An ERP is just one very expensive place to run that check.

"The step that actually breaks the match isn't software. It's the goods receipt. When the dock signs the packing slip and finance never sees it, every invoice becomes a manual exception."

Rebuilding the process instead of buying the ERP is a 2-to-4-week job — same control, without the 11-month implementation or the six-figure line item. The full 5-step version is written specifically for Controllers running QuickBooks Enterprise, Sage Intacct, or Business Central.

1
Lock the PO first
No purchase goes out without an approved PO with a line-item price and quantity
  
2
Fix the goods receipt
Every receipt gets logged by finance — not just the dock — the day it arrives
3
Match before payment
All three documents — PO, receipt, invoice — must align before any payment is released
  
4
Define your tolerance
Set a variance threshold (e.g. ±2%) so small discrepancies auto-clear and only real exceptions escalate

● From the CEO's Desk

Sachin Sharma

Founder & CEO, ProcureDesk

By the time a budget overrun shows up at month-end, it's already too late to stop it. I hear this regularly from CFOs and controllers at manufacturing and construction firms processing 200-plus purchase orders a month. The variance report lands, the conversation gets uncomfortable, everyone agrees to be more careful next month — and then it happens again. What nobody traces back is where the overrun actually started. It wasn't at month-end. It was three weeks earlier, when a purchase request was approved with no real-time budget check attached to it. A proactive budget check stops that at the source: the moment the request is created, the system checks available budget in real time. If the funds aren't there, the request stops or escalates before anyone has committed to anything. The awkward month-end conversation disappears because the process stopped approving spend that had no budget behind it.

● Operator's Checklist

The Month-End Close Checklist: AP and PO Side

Most month-end close problems aren't accounting problems — they're procurement problems that finance inherits. Run through this list starting five days before close.

01Pull all open POs older than 30 days. If a PO has been open more than a month with no goods receipt, it's either stuck, forgotten, or about to create a surprise accrual.
02Identify every invoice sitting in an approval queue. Anything not approved by Day -2 will either miss the close or force a manual journal entry. Neither is good.
03Check your GR/NI account for unmatched receipts. Goods received but not invoiced create accruals that quietly inflate your balance sheet. Every line needs a matching invoice or a close date.
04Verify budget vs. actuals for every cost center. Don't wait for the variance report to find out a department ran over. Pull the comparison now while there's still time to ask questions.
05Confirm all invoices over $5,000 have a matched PO. If an invoice arrived without a PO, you either have a compliance gap or a vendor who skipped the process. Both need to be resolved before close.
06Lock purchasing for the final 48 hours. Any new requests submitted in the last two days will almost certainly miss the close. Set a hard cutoff and communicate it to every department head.
07Document every manual adjustment made during close. Recurring manual entries are a process problem, not a one-time fix. Logging them gives you the evidence to make the case for automation next quarter.

● The Call-Out

💡 This week's take

Unauthorized spending isn't a people problem. It's a process gap.

When an employee buys something outside of the approved workflow, the instinct is to blame the employee. Write a policy. Send a reminder. Schedule a training. None of it sticks — because the real problem isn't that people don't know the rules. It's that following the rules is slower and harder than just placing the order.

A 4-step pre-purchase workflow — request, budget check, approval, PO — removes the shortcut. When the process is faster than the workaround, people use the process. When the workaround is faster, they don't. Email approvals break because they have no enforcement layer. A no-PO-no-payment rule works because it doesn't rely on anyone's goodwill.
