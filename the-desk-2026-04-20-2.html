How to Save 3–5% Without Cutting Anything

Most procurement teams are losing savings they already negotiated. Not because of bad vendors — because purchases keep bypassing the process.

📅 Monday, April 28  ·  2 PM EST

You negotiated the contracts. You set the preferred vendors. You built the approval workflow. And then someone bought $8,000 of software on a corporate card — and you found out when the invoice arrived. That gap between what gets approved and what actually gets bought is where 3–5% of your annual spend quietly disappears. It's not a vendor problem. It's a process problem that looks like a savings problem.

"The issue isn't that teams overpay on contracts. It's that those contracts get bypassed — and by the time finance sees it, the spend is already done."

On April 28, we'll walk through four patterns that drain procurement savings — and the specific fixes for each one. You'll leave with a framework you can take back to your team the same week. No slides about why procurement matters. Just the leaks and how to close them.

● Operator's Checklist

7 Signs Your Procurement Savings Are Leaking Through the Process

You don't lose 3–5% all at once. It leaks through small gaps — gaps that look normal until you add them up. Check these before Friday.

01Pull your last 30 days of invoices without a matching PO. If more than 10% arrived without a prior approval, you have a pre-approval gap — and those purchases cost more than the ones that went through the process.
02Count how many vendors you've paid in the last quarter. If the number surprises you, you've lost control of vendor consolidation — and with it, every volume discount you negotiated.
03Check whether your preferred vendor list is actually being used. Ask three department heads which vendor they last used for a recurring category. If the answers vary, your preferred vendor program is a policy on paper, not in practice.
04Find out how many people can approve purchases on a corporate card with no prior PO. Each one of those people is a potential bypass point — and a potential spend report surprise at month-end.
05Look at how long your average approval cycle takes. If it's over 48 hours, employees aren't waiting — they're working around it. Slow approvals don't create compliance, they create shadow purchasing.
06Check when your spend reports are available relative to when spend happens. If your controller is reconciling last month's purchases this month, you don't have visibility — you have history. That's too late to act on.
07Calculate what percentage of your spend is truly captured in your procurement system. If it's below 80%, the savings you think you're generating on paper are being eroded by the 20% that bypasses the process entirely.

● From the CEO's Desk
S

Sachin Sharma

Founder & CEO, ProcureDesk

Early in my career, every major vendor negotiation had one goal: get as much as possible — squeeze every line item, push on payment terms, win every point. Then the contract would start, and operations would need more scope than what was priced. The vendor had nothing left to be flexible with, so every change order came back expensive. We paid it all back, just at a worse rate. A mentor eventually gave me a principle I've used ever since: the best negotiations happen when both parties feel like they left something on the table. We started identifying upfront which terms were non-negotiable for us and which ones we were willing to give on. We let the vendor win something on purpose. What we got back was flexibility when we needed it — faster response times, favorable scope changes, a vendor who actually wanted the relationship to work. For your strategic vendors, a sustainable relationship is worth more than the last dollar you can squeeze out of a contract. The change orders always come.
